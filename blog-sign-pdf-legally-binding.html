Are typed-or-drawn signatures on a PDF actually legally binding?

March 22, 2026 · 6 min read

You drag a PDF into a signing tool, draw your signature with the trackpad, click Apply, and email it back. Is that signature legally binding?

The honest answer: usually yes, in most countries, for most everyday documents. But there are nuances worth understanding before you use a drawn signature for something important.

None of this is legal advice. For high-stakes contracts, consult an actual lawyer in your jurisdiction.

Three levels of "electronic signature"

Most countries' e-signature laws (EU eIDAS, US ESIGN, India IT Act 2000, etc.) recognize a hierarchy:

Simple Electronic Signature (SES)

This is what you get from a signature pad or a typed name in an email. It's an electronic mark indicating intent to sign. Legally valid for most everyday agreements — leases, employment offers, vendor contracts, NDAs.

Advanced Electronic Signature (AES)

An SES that's uniquely linked to the signer, can identify them, and can detect if the document was modified after signing. This usually involves a digital certificate from a trusted authority. DocuSign Premium and Adobe Sign produce these.

Qualified Electronic Signature (QES)

An AES created using a "qualified signature creation device" and a certificate from a "qualified trust service provider." Legally equivalent to a handwritten signature in the EU, with the strongest enforceability. Required for some specific document types (real estate transactions in some countries, certain government filings).

What law applies where

European Union (eIDAS Regulation)

All three signature levels are recognized. Even an SES "shall not be denied legal effect ... solely on the grounds that it is in electronic form" (Article 25). For most everyday contracts, an SES is enough.

United States (ESIGN Act + UETA)

The 2000 ESIGN Act and the state-level UETA make electronic signatures legally equivalent to handwritten ones for most documents. Exceptions include wills, family law documents (divorce, adoption), some real estate, court orders, and notices about utility shutoffs.

India (Information Technology Act 2000)

Recognizes electronic signatures as valid. Section 5 grants legal recognition to digital signatures specifically authenticated through licensed Certifying Authorities. For SES (drawn or typed), enforceability is generally good but case law is still evolving.

UK (eIDAS Regulation, post-Brexit)

The UK retained the EU eIDAS framework after Brexit, so the same three-tier system applies.

What makes a signature stick

Beyond the legal framework, courts look for evidence that the person actually signed. The strength of your e-signature depends on:

  • Intent — did the signer mean to sign? A drawn signature in a PDF clearly demonstrates intent. A name typed at the end of an email might not, depending on context.
  • Identity — can you prove it was them? An IP-address log, a phone-confirmed signature, or an account-based signing flow strengthens this.
  • Attribution — is the signature linked to the document such that it can't be moved to a different one? Embedded PDF signatures are good; an image of a signature pasted in is weaker.
  • Tamper-evidence — can you prove the document wasn't changed after signing? A digital certificate provides this; a drawn signature alone doesn't.

When a drawn signature is fine

  • NDAs with vendors
  • Employment offer letters
  • Internal HR documents (PTO, expense reports)
  • Service agreements between businesses where both parties are signing willingly
  • Personal contracts where parties trust each other

When you want a stronger signature

  • Real estate transactions (some jurisdictions require notarization)
  • Wills and estate documents
  • Major financial agreements (mortgages, large loans)
  • Government filings that specifically require qualified signatures
  • Contracts with high dispute likelihood

For these, use a service that produces an Advanced or Qualified signature with a verified identity audit trail (DocuSign, Adobe Sign, Aadhaar e-Sign in India, etc.).
