PDF vs DOCX: which one should you actually send?

March 28, 2026 · 5 min read

You finish drafting a contract. You go to send it. You pause. PDF or Word? Whatever you pick, you'll be wrong about half the time. Here's a clearer way to decide.

The fundamental difference

PDF was designed in 1993 to solve one problem: making documents look identical everywhere. A PDF you create on a Mac in 2026 will look pixel-identical when opened on a Windows machine in 2050.

DOCX (Microsoft Word's format) was designed for the opposite goal: editable, structured text that flows and reflows depending on the device, fonts, and version of Word. It's brilliant for collaboration. It's terrible for pixel-perfect fidelity.

Neither format is "better." They solve different problems, and choosing wrong creates friction.

Use PDF when…

  • The document is final. No more edits expected. Send the PDF.
  • You care about exact layout. Resumes, invoices, contracts, presentations — anything where typography and spacing matter.
  • The recipient might not have Word. PDF readers are universal. Word is not.
  • You need legal evidence of content. PDFs are harder to invisibly modify than DOCX files.
  • You're submitting to anyone official. Government, court, university — they want PDFs.
  • You want to prevent edits. Not bulletproof, but the friction of editing a PDF is much higher than editing a DOCX.
  • You're sending a fillable form. PDF forms are standard; DOCX forms are flaky.

Use DOCX when…

  • The document is still being edited. Drafts, work-in-progress documents, things you'll iterate on.
  • You want collaborators to suggest changes. Track Changes is the killer feature here.
  • You're using Word's specific tools. Mail merge, mail-out templates, complex form letters.
  • The recipient definitely has Word and will open it natively.
  • The document needs to reflow on different screens. A long report being read on a phone benefits from DOCX's fluidity.

Common mistakes

Sending a DOCX as final delivery

You spent hours getting the formatting right. You send the .docx. The recipient opens it on a different version of Word with different default fonts, and your beautiful layout falls apart. Always export to PDF for final delivery.

Sending a PDF when you want feedback

"Please review and let me know your thoughts" attached to a PDF is a recipe for confusion. The recipient can't easily edit, comment requires a paid tool for many people, and any feedback they send back will be in a separate document. Send DOCX, get cleaner reviews.

Forgetting that PDFs can be edited

"It's a PDF, so they can't change anything." Wrong. Free tools can edit any PDF. If you genuinely need un-editable, you need a digital signature or password-protected PDF — and even those have caveats.

Locking yourself out of a PDF

You converted everything to PDF and now you need to update the address on page 4. If you don't have the original DOCX, you're stuck. Always keep the source file. Tools like our PDF to Word converter can recover text, but layout fidelity is hit-or-miss.

The hybrid workflow

The professional flow is: write in Word/Google Docs/whatever, iterate as DOCX, export to PDF for final distribution. Keep both files. The DOCX is your source of truth (and your insurance if you need to make edits later). The PDF is what the world sees.

What about Markdown? Pages? Notion?

Same principle: editor format vs. delivery format. Use whatever you're productive in for the editing phase. Export to PDF for the delivery phase. The world has standardized on PDF for finished documents — fighting that just creates friction.

Bottom line

  • Final, formal, fixed? PDF.
  • Editable, collaborative, evolving? DOCX.
  • Always keep your DOCX source. Even after you send the PDF.
