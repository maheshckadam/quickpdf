How to compress a PDF without making it look terrible

April 15, 2026 · 5 min read

You email a PDF and your boss replies "Can you make this smaller?" You run it through a compressor, send it back, and now the text is fuzzy and the screenshots look like they were taken with a flip phone. There's a better way.

What actually makes PDFs big

Before compressing, it's worth understanding where the bytes are coming from. PDFs typically grow large for one of three reasons:

  1. Embedded images — by far the most common cause. A high-resolution photo can be 5+ MB on its own.
  2. Embedded fonts — every font used in the PDF is bundled into the file. Fonts with many glyphs (Chinese, Japanese) can add megabytes.
  3. Scanned pages — these are technically just images, but they're a special case because every page is a full-page image.

Text-only PDFs are usually small. PDFs with photos, scans, or many fonts are usually big.

The two strategies for compression

1. Lossless compression (small wins, no quality loss)

This recompresses already-compressed streams using better algorithms, removes duplicate data, and strips unused metadata. Savings are modest — maybe 10-30% — but the output looks identical to the original.

2. Lossy compression (big wins, some quality loss)

This re-encodes images at lower quality, downsamples to lower DPI, and converts color images to grayscale where possible. Savings can be dramatic — 50-90% smaller. The cost is visible quality loss in images.

Most "compress PDF" tools (including ours) are some flavor of lossy. The trick is choosing the right level for your use case.

Choosing the right compression level

For email attachments

Medium quality is usually fine. Modern email clients display images at relatively low resolution anyway. The recipient is going to skim, not zoom in.

For printed documents

Use high quality. Even if the PDF will only be printed once, 300 DPI is standard for print and you want the source to support it.

For archival or legal use

Don't compress at all, or use a true lossless tool. Future-you might want to zoom into that document and see what it actually said. PDF/A archival format exists for exactly this reason.

For web display

Aggressive compression is fine. Browsers display at screen resolution; nobody zooms past 200%. Get the file as small as possible so it loads fast.

The "looks terrible" trap

If you've ever compressed a PDF and ended up with text that looks slightly blurry, here's what happened: the text was originally rasterized into the document as part of an image (common with scans). When the image got recompressed at lower quality, the text — being part of the image — got blurred too.

The fix: run the PDF through OCR first to convert the text-as-image back into actual text characters. Then compression only affects images, and text stays sharp.

What we do under the hood

Quickpdf's Compress PDF tool re-renders each page to a JPEG at adjustable quality, then packs the JPEGs back into a PDF. This works very well for image-heavy PDFs and scans, less well for pure-text PDFs (where there's not much to compress beyond what's already optimal).

You can choose three quality levels:

  • Low (50% JPEG quality, 1× scale) — smallest output, visible quality loss
  • Medium (70% quality, 1.5× scale) — good balance for most uses
  • High (85% quality, 2× scale) — gentle compression, minimal quality loss

Run all three on a sample PDF and compare. You'll quickly see which level fits your needs.
